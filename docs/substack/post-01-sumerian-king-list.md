Let me show you the Weld-Blundell Prism.

It's a four-sided baked clay tablet, about 8 inches tall. It sits in the Ashmolean Museum in Oxford. And it contains **the most dangerous list of names in archaeology.**

It's called the **Sumerian King List.** And its first few lines read like science fiction:

> *"When kingship descended from heaven,*

28,800 years. For **one king.**

Before the flood, the list says, **eight kings** ruled for a total of **241,200 years.**

Then the flood swept over. And after it? Suddenly reign lengths drop to hundreds, then decades, then normal human lifespans.

## What Scholars Say

Mainstream Assyriologists have a different answer.

The numbers are **base-60 conventions.** 28,800 = 60 × 60 × 8. 36,000 = 60 × 60 × 10. The Sumerians loved round sexagesimal numbers. It's a literary device — not a historical ledger.

The purpose of the list? **Political propaganda.** Later kings traced their lineage to legendary pre-flood rulers. It legitimized their rule. "I come from a 200,000-year-old line of divinely-appointed kings. You must obey me."

No evidence for a 3,600-year planet. No evidence for "Anunnaki years." The numbers follow Mesopotamian numerical patterns — not astronomical ones.

The exactness. The precision. The *weirdness.*

Every alternative explanation — base-60, symbolism, propaganda — explains *what* the numbers mean. But none of them fully explains **why they chose those specific numbers.**
